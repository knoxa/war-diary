Account of the Action of the 11th INFANTRY BRIGADE on the 26th August 1914 between FONTAINE-AU-PIRE and LIGNY-EN-CAMBRESIS West of LE CATEAU.

After a continuous night march from BRIASTRE 1½ miles S. of SOLESMES, the 11th Infantry Brigade halted for 1½ hours at the northern end of FONTAINE-AU-PIRE at 2-45 am on 26th August 1914. At 4-14 am as the brigade was about to resume its march, rifle, machine gun and shell fire was offered on the outposts WEST of the village of BEAUVOIS. While the Rifle Brigade held off this attack from the N. and N.W, the Somerset L I were pushed forward into the Southern end of BEAUVOIS to hold off the enemy to the N.E. and to ensure the safe passage of all wheeled vehicles to the EAST and then to the SOUTH, the direct roads South from the halting place being impassable owing to the wet. Under cover of these two Battalions, the other two Battalions of the Brigade moved SOUTH and took up a position on the ridge of which CARRIÈRES (the Quarry) ½ mile S.W. of the village of FONTAINE-AU-PIRE is the centre. All the transport was successfully got away, but owing to the necessity of having to stick to the roads, the 1st Line Transport had to go a long way round before it could again approach the Infantry. The 2nd Line Transport was ordered back to SELVINGLY, which it reached safely in due course. Under cover of the fire of the 1st Bn Hampshire Regt the two advanced Battalions retired on LA CARRIÈRE. The Somerset L.I. (1½ Coys.) occupying the Eastern end of the Quarry while a company of the E. LANCS occupied the Western end. The Somersets prolonged their own line from LA CARRIÈRE to the S.E, sending two companys back towards LIGNY to entrench a position N. of that town. The three remaining Coys. of the E. LANCS also retired to the slope N. of LIGNY. W. and S.W. of CARRIÈRE the line was taken up by the Hampshires whose left rested on the edge of the plateau. S. of the Railway line, the Brigade having thus established itself on the CARRIÈRE Ridge, the Coys. of the E. LANCS and Somersets were recalled from their covering positions N. of LIGNY and formed local reserves on the WEST and EAST flanks of the CARRIÈRE position. Against the 11th Inf. Bde, the enemy concentrated a very heavy fire both from field guns and machine guns. The Rifle fire was not heavy as the Cavalry and afterwards the Infantry kept at a respectful distance and did not venture to show themselves on the smooth glacis-like slope which lay in front of the position. The hostile Infantry however worked round both flanks, attacking from the N.W. along the line of the Railway and on the N.E. establishing themselves in the Southern outskirts of the village. The enemy fire was uninterrupted and severe, and on four occassions caused the troops in the more advanced positions to retire, on each occassion though, they were either led back or replaced by fresh troops and the position was successfully maintained. At about 3 p.m. the 5th Div. having returned from CAUDRY, it was seen that to hold the CARRIÈRES position any longer would lead to the Brigade being taken in reverse. In accordance therefore with Divisional instructions the Brigade made preparation to retire to LIGNY. This retirement was carefully organised with close co-operation between the Infantry and Artillery, which latter took up positions in and to the W. of LIGNY, ready to engage enemy's Artillery and Infantry to cover our retirement. The Infantry, with the Rifle Brigade left on the position as a rearguard, was formed up in lines on the dead ground S. of the RAVIN WARNELLE, whence on the command being given it moved off in good order towards the village of LIGNY. Each Battalion was given a separate objective viz:- Hampshire to W. of village; E LANCS to right centre of village; Somersets to E end of village; the Rifle Brigade following towards the left centre. The shrapnel fire on our troops was very severe, notwithstanding the support given by our guns. There were a good number of casualties but considering the way the whole ground was plastered with shrapnel bullets, it was surprising that the casualties were not greater. On arrival at LIGNY, the Battalions reformed in the localities that had been alloted to them and took up the defense of the front edge of their respective portion of the village. As soon as the rearguard quitted the CARRIÈRES position the German Infantry, which up to that time had kept themselves carefully concealed, advanced in large numbers on the CAUDRY side of that position. They afforded excellent targets to our Artillery and their formations were speedily broken up by our shrapnel fire. Shortly after the Brigade had established itself at LIGNY an attack was made on the N.E. and E. of the village by German Infantry. This attack was pushed in twice, but on each occasion was repulsed with heavy loss. Shortly before 5 p.m. the Brigade was ordered to retire via CAULLERY toward MALINCOURT. This retirement having to be conducted under heavy artillery fire the formation adopted was that of small columns of fours each as far as possible under an officer. After passing CAULLERY the enemy's pursuit by Artillery fire died away owing as we afterwards learned to the attack on the Germans from the N.W. by a French Corps. In connection with the above very trying operations, I should like to bring to notice the good services of the following Officers, N.C.O's and men.

Bucy Le Long, 16 Sept 1914

The above officers, NCOs & men are those to whom, in my opinion, we most owe it that the 11th Inf Brig held on for ten hours to its dominating position S.W of FONTAINE-AU-PIRE, the highest point of the CAUDRY - CREVE COURT ridge.

The position was attacked from W-N-W south of the railway right round by the north to the E-N-E south of the village of FONTAINE-AU-PIRE, and the fire was so severe that the men in the firing lines, after heavy losses in leaders and men, retired precipitantly on four occasions.

It was only by the personal example of the Officers and NCOs that they, or fresh troops, were led forward again to re-occupy the firing line.

If this position, which was unsupported by the fire of other troops had been evacuated early in the day, the results of the consequent advance of the German enveloping attack from the N.W. could not have been other than disastrous to the remainder of our army. I therefore recommend them all for special recognition. My reasons for each are as under:-

Maj Rickman commanded the companies of the Rif Brig supporting the front line in the Quarry Pits (CARRIERES). By his excellent dispositions and confident manner, he contributed more than any other individual to the success in holding this part of the position. He was in command of the rearguard when the Eleventh Brigade retired from CARRIERE, and was very seriously wonded and left behind.

Capt Boyd. Perhaps the finiest young soldier I have met. A professional soldier of strong character, is a first rate Staff Officer and would make a capable commander. He is a gentleman, who got his commission through the ranks and is therefore old for his rank. I recommend that he be promoted into some regiment as Major and given a Brevet Lieut-Colonelcy.

Capt Hon L.C. Palk was in command of the company in the very exposed firing line S.W. of (CARRIERES). He commanded his company well and was largely influential in maintaining our position in this post.

Maj Salmon, Capt Brownlow, Lieut Campbell. These Officers led their company forward into the firing line in the Quarry Pits after these quarries had twice been evacuated by other troops, owing to the heavy fire. This company maintained its position in the quarry until the end.

Col Jackson was in command of the batt'n which was the left of our front line. He was throughout the day (and throughout the campaign until he got wounded gallantly directing his men) a capable and gallant leader.

Lt Col Le Marchant. A capable battalion commander and a gallant man. (Afterwards killed in action at LA FERTE).

Maj Prowse, Capt Connellan, Capt Jones-Mortimer, Capt Hon Morgan-Grenville. Five exceptionally capable officers who proved themselves good leaders in the field. The early advancement of these officers would be of benefit to the Army.

Capt Freestun did valuable work as my Staff Capt during the fight and took over the duties of Brig Maj after Capt Boyd was incapacitated by his wound.

Retirement of 11th Infantry Brigade from Le Cateau

26th August 1914

You probably know that the 11th Brigade pretty well all right spent the night after Le Cateau at Serain (i.e. Wednesday night 26th/27th), the Brigade headquarters being the Padré's house, which I visited years after, and the old man rememembered it all well. We marched early next morning across country to Beaurevoir and thens more or less as shown on the map to Nauroy where we put out some protective people an proceeded to get some food for the men. Here I met some of the Royal Scots under Duncan (now Major-General). All seemed quiet, and H.W. [Hunter Weston] and myself, with the machine-gun officer (Ovey), went to have some grub at the Chateau in the northern end of the town (now in ruin). I was rather bad about this time, as my wound was hurting a lot. Suddenly a lot of firing, shelling by light guns, evidently H.A. broke out, and H.W. and Ovey (who took my pony) dashed off out of the house, leaving me to make my way out into the orchard with not much on, as Madame laComptesse was just playing about with my shoulder. When I got out, some of the Irish Horse dashed by, going hell for leather, and I luckily bumped into a party of the Rifle Bde. under poor Prittie, retiring steadily. I joined up with them and we picked up the rest of the Company on the Way through the village, and on clearing the village moved in the direction of Bellincourt, through this village and then steadily on towards Tertry, which we reached in the evening, and there got in touch with the Division again. Between Bellincourt and Vendelles (see black line on the map), we picked up about

  • 2 Coys. Somerset L.I. under Prowse, or more
  • 2 Coys. Rifle Bde.
  • 2 Coys. Hants, under Palk I think
  • some E. Lancs and oddments.

So we reached Tertry eventually, following the line on the map, with a good part of the Brigade fairly all right. Put out outposts there, and it was arranged that the guns passing Tertry that night on their way S.W. should pick up as many men as they could. You remember the rest.

As far as I remember, poor old Prowse was very good all this time, but the men were very tired. I managed to get in a country cart with some straw in it, and we met H-W and the remainder of the Brigade when we reached the 4th Division some time next day at - I forget the place.

I should say we had about 1,000 men at Tertry, may be more.

I send you also a few old messages which may be useful. Send me them and the maps back when done with. The Colonel of the Hampshires was wounded outside Nauroy, and taken prisoner.
